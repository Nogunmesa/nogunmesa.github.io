What is Identity?

Identity is a complex and dynamic concept. It includes the labels we assign ourselves and those that society assigns to us. Identity is shaped by various factors, such as race, ethnicity, gender, class, sexual identity, ability, and culture. These characteristics intersect to form our unique eperience in the world. Understanding identity is key to understanding the diversity of human experience. Every person’s identity shapes how they see themselves and how others interact with them.

The identity wheel illustrates the different aspects of identity that intersect to create our unique experiences. Consider how these factors influence your worldview and interactions.

Understanding Intersectionality

What is Intersectionality?Intersectionality refers to the way different aspects of a person's identity combine to create unique experiences of discrimination and privilege. For example, the experience of a 'black woman' is different from that of a 'white woman' or a 'black man' because her identity is shaped by both her race and her gender.

Intersectionality highlights that we can’t fully understand issues like racism or sexism unless we look at how they intersect. This concept is critical to promoting equity and inclusivity in diverse spaces.

Introduction of the Term

The term "intersectionality" was coined by Kimberlé Crenshaw in 1989 to describe how various forms of inequality and identity intersect.

Growth of Feminist Theory

In the 1990s, intersectionality became a critical aspect of feminist theory, highlighting the interconnectedness of gender, race, and class.

Legal Recognition

Courts began to recognize intersectionality in cases involving discrimination, leading to more nuanced understandings of injustice.

Global Movements

Global movements like Black Lives Matter highlighted intersectionality in social justice, focusing on how various identities affect experiences of oppression.

Continued Relevance

Today, intersectionality remains essential in discussions about equity and justice, influencing policies and practices across various fields.

Case Study: Combahee River Collective

The Combahee River Collective was a group of Black feminists who advocated for both racial and gender equality, emphasizing the importance of addressing multiple forms of oppression.

Ethics, Cultural Competence, and Inclusion

Ethics is about making decisions that respect the dignity and rights of others. In diverse settings, ethical decision-making requires cultural competence—understanding the cultural backgrounds and experiences of others and how these affect interactions.

Diversity and inclusion go hand-in-hand with ethics. Organizations that value diversity aim to include different perspectives in decision-making, ensuring that all voices are heard. But diversity isn’t just about having different people in the room; it’s about creating an environment where those differences are respected and celebrated.

Cultural competence involves recognizing our own biases, actively listening, and continually educating ourselves about cultures and identities that are different from our own.

The Ethical Decision-Making Model

1. Recognizing Bias

Identify personal and systemic biases that may affect your decision-making process.

2. Considering the Context

Examine the context of the decision, including the stakeholders involved and potential impacts.

3. Making a Decision

Weigh the options available and make a decision that aligns with ethical principles.

4. Reflecting on Outcomes

After implementing the decision, reflect on its outcomes and learn from the experience.

Historical Trends & Organizational Culture

Historical events have shaped how organizations function and how technology has evolved. Understanding these trends is key to fostering inclusive organizational cultures.

How History Shapes the Present

History has had a profound impact on organizational cultures and the development of technology. From exclusionary practices in hiring to the development of tools that only cater to certain groups, these trends have affected who gets to succeed and who is left behind.

One example is the role of women in technology. Historically excluded from STEM fields, women have fought for a place at the table, changing the landscape of science and innovation today.

Key Moments in History Affecting Workplace Diversity

Civil Rights Movement

The Civil Rights Movement led to significant legal and societal changes that increased opportunities for marginalized groups.

Title IX

Passed in 1972, Title IX prohibits gender discrimination in educational programs and activities, impacting women in STEM.

Affirmative Action Policies

Affirmative action policies were implemented to promote diversity and counteract historical exclusion in the workplace.

Me Too Movement

The Me Too movement raised awareness about sexual harassment and led to increased advocacy for women's rights in various sectors.

Google's Diversity Initiatives

Google has implemented various diversity initiatives to address historical exclusion and foster a more inclusive tech environment.
